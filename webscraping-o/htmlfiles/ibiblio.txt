        <h2 class="entry-title">
         <a href="https://www.ibiblio.org/archive/2021/03/record-archives/" rel="bookmark">
          Record Archives
         </a>
        </h2>
       </header>
       <!-- .entry-header -->
       <div class="entry-content">
        <p>
         <span style="font-weight: 400;">
          I recently discovered this
          <a href="http://www.littlewonderrecords.com/">
           Little Wonder Records Archive
          </a>
          , an archive of 5 ½ ” about 90 second long records created between 1914 and 1923 to showcase the popular music of the time in a format that more people could afford.
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         <span style="font-weight: 400;">
          ibiblio hosts a few recording archives as well.
         </span>
        </p>
        <h5>
         <a href="https://www.ibiblio.org/catalog/items/show/4055">
          Pandora Records Archive
         </a>
        </h5>
        <p>
         <span style="font-weight: 400;">
          The Pandora Records archive contains classical music from Pandora Records, organized by instrument. Recordings can be found under the mp3 and vorbis links. Pandora Records was extant for about 10 years, starting in 1973.
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         <span style="font-weight: 400;">
          The files are not labeled with the artist – but
          <a href="http://www.ibiblio.org/pandora/LIST.html">
           this list
          </a>
          may shed some light on who plays what
         </span>
         <span style="font-weight: 400;">
          . Here’s
          <a href="http://www.ibiblio.org/pandora/mp3/2_pianos/pan116a/Rachmaninoff1.mp3">
           Rachmaninov on 2 pianos
          </a>
          likely from Neal and Nancy O’Doan.
         </span>
        </p>
        <h5>
         <a href="https://www.ibiblio.org/catalog/items/show/3587">
          Goldband Recording Archive
         </a>
        </h5>
        <p>
         <span style="font-weight: 400;">
          At one time we hosted the Goldband Recording Archive for the Southern Folklife Collection – now hosted at their own site
          <a href="https://finding-aids.lib.unc.edu/20245/">
           here
          </a>
         </span>
         <span style="font-weight: 400;">
          .
          <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goldband_Records">
           Goldband Records
          </a>
          was based in Lake Charles, Louisiana and was active in the 1930’s and 40’s. This archive is real treasure trove of photos and recordings from the era in country and western, cajun, and swamp pop music.
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         Some notable examples of what you can find there
        </p>
        <p>
         <span style="font-weight: 400;">
          Dolly Parton recorded her first original song “Puppy Love” at Goldband.
          <br/>
          <a href="https://dc.lib.unc.edu/cdm/singleitem/collection/sfc/id/40911/rec/1">
           https://dc.lib.unc.edu/cdm/singleitem/collection/sfc/id/40911/rec/1
          </a>
          <br/>
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         <span style="font-weight: 400;">
          Cajun accordion of Moise Robin – Here’s a great
          <a href="https://arhoolie.org/moise-robin/">
           interview
          </a>
          with him from 1980.
          <br/>
          <a href="https://dc.lib.unc.edu/cdm/singleitem/collection/sfc/id/40075/rec/1">
           https://dc.lib.unc.edu/cdm/singleitem/collection/sfc/id/40075/rec/1
          </a>
          <br/>
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         <span style="font-weight: 400;">
          Recordings of
          <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phil_Phillips">
           Phil Philips
          </a>
          (
          <a href="https://64parishes.org/entry/swamp-pop-music">
           Swamp Pop
          </a>
          legend who wrote Sea of Love) singing Stormy Weather and Take This Heart.
         </span>
         <a href="https://dc.lib.unc.edu/cdm/singleitem/collection/sfc/id/40937/rec/1">
          <span style="font-weight: 400;">
           https://dc.lib.unc.edu/cdm/singleitem/collection/sfc/id/40937/rec/1
          </span>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <span style="font-weight: 400;">
          Zydeco legend
          <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boozoo_Chavis">
           Boozoo Chavis
          </a>
         </span>
         <a href="https://dc.lib.unc.edu/cdm/singleitem/collection/sfc/id/40300/rec/1">
          <span style="font-weight: 400;">
           https://dc.lib.unc.edu/cdm/singleitem/collection/sfc/id/40300/rec/1
          </span>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <span style="font-weight: 400;">
          This recording is labeled with the names
         </span>
         <a href="https://www.fatsdominoofficial.com/">
          Antoine “Fats” Domino,
         </a>
         his mentor
         <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dave_Bartholomew">
          Dave Bartholomew
         </a>
         , Travis, and
         <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Katie_Webster">
          Katie Webster
         </a>
         has some great early Rock and Roll and Swamp Pop hits:
        </p>
        <p>
         <span style="font-weight: 400;">
          Katie Webster’s Don’t Drive Me Deeper
          <br/>
          Fats Domino’s Blueberry Hill
          <br/>
          Fats Domino and Dave Bartholomew’s Ain’t That a Shame
          <br/>
          Fats Domino and Dave Bartholomew’s Valley of Tears
          <br/>
          Dave Bartholomew’s Blue Monday
          <br/>
         </span>
         <a href="https://dc.lib.unc.edu/cdm/singleitem/collection/sfc/id/40805/rec/1">
          <span style="font-weight: 400;">
           https://dc.lib.unc.edu/cdm/singleitem/collection/sfc/id/40805/rec/1
          </span>
         </a>
        </p>
       </div>

       <header class="entry-header">
        <h2 class="entry-title">
         <a href="https://www.ibiblio.org/archive/2020/12/carl-von-linne/" rel="bookmark">
          Carl von Linné
         </a>
        </h2>
       </header>
       <!-- .entry-header -->
       <div class="entry-content">
        <p>
         <span style="font-weight: 400;">
          In 1753
          <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus">
           Carl Linnaeus (Carl von Linné
          </a>
          ) set out to organize and classify every plant known to man at the time. His work was published in four volumes, Species Plantarum, in sections I – XIII. Thanks to
          <a href="https://www.ibiblio.org/catalog/items/show/3927">
           Project Gutenberg
          </a>
          , you can read them all  for free in their original Latin (Google translate may help):
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         <span style="font-weight: 400;">
          <a href="https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/20771">
           Species Plantarum, Sections I-III
          </a>
          <br/>
         </span>
         <span style="font-weight: 400;">
          <a href="https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/27049">
           Species Plantarum, Sections IV-V
          </a>
          <br/>
         </span>
         <span style="font-weight: 400;">
          <a href="https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/30104">
           Species Plantarum, Sections VI-X
          </a>
          <br/>
         </span>
         <span style="font-weight: 400;">
          <a href="https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/31010">
           Species Plantarum, Sections XI-XIII
          </a>
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         <span style="font-weight: 400;">
          More accessible to read may be Linnaeus’ journal of his
          <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expedition_to_Lapland">
           1732 trip to Lapland
          </a>
          in Northern Sweden, also available at
          <a href="https://www.gutenberg.org/">
           Project Gutenberg
          </a>
          :
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         <span style="font-weight: 400;">
          <a href="https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/34779">
           Lachesis Lapponica; Or, A Tour in Lapland, Volume 1
          </a>
          <br/>
         </span>
         <span style="font-weight: 400;">
          <a href="https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/36059">
           Lachesis Lapponica; Or, A Tour in Lapland, Volume 2
          </a>
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         Beautiful descriptions of Lapland in 1732 in the spring, still snow on the ground in some places, but early spring flowers starting to come up.
        </p>
        <p>
         Those interested in plants and classification can find more information at the
         <a href="https://www.ibiblio.org/pic/index.htm">
          Plant Information Center website
         </a>
         from the
         <a href="http://herbarium.unc.edu/">
          UNC Herbarium
         </a>
         .
        </p>
       </div>
