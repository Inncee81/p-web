        <h2 class="entry-title">
         <a href="https://quoteinvestigator.com/2021/03/15/scene/" rel="bookmark">
          All the Love Scenes Had Been Shot Like Murder Scenes, and All the Murder Scenes Like Love Scenes
         </a>
        </h2>
       </header>
       <!-- .entry-header -->
       <div class="entry-content">
        <p>
         <strong>
          Alfred Hitchcock? François Truffaut? Grace Kelly? Sam Mendes?
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Dear Quote Investigator:
         </strong>
         Director Alfred Hitchcock was a master of suspense and horror. For decades his filmmaking techniques have been closely studied by other directors, screenwriters, and critics. One observer uncovered a disquieting connection between Hitchcock’s portrayal of homicide and intimacy:
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          The murder scenes are filmed like love scenes, and the love scenes are filmed like murder scenes.
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <p>
         This assertion has been attributed to fellow director François Truffaut, popular actress Grace Kelly, and Alfred Hitchcock himself. Would you please explore this topic?
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Quote Investigator:
         </strong>
         In 1974 a gala honoring Hitchcock was held at the Lincoln Center in New York. A series of film scenes from Hitchcock were shown to attendees during the evening. The clips were arranged into different categories, e.g., chase, love, suspense, catastrophe, and murder. When François Truffaut saw those extracts he developed his thesis connecting scenes of mayhem and amour. In May 1976 the Canadian film magazine “Take One” published a piece by Truffaut. Boldface added to excerpts by
         <strong>
          QI
         </strong>
         :
         <a class="simple-footnote" href="#note-439330-1" id="return-note-439330-1" title="1976 May 21, Take One, Volume 5, Number 2, Alfred Hitchcock: A Friendly Salute, Subsection: Hitchcock in 1976 by François Truffaut, Start Page 43, Quote Page 44, Unicorn Publishing, Montreal, Canada. (Verified with scans; accessed via HathiTrust)">
          <sup>
           1
          </sup>
         </a>
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          What struck me that evening while reseeing all of these little bits of films I know by heart (isolated from their context and put together for a single evening) was the simultaneous sincerity and savagery of the Hitchcockian oeuvre. I realized that
          <strong>
           all the love scenes had been shot like murder scenes, and all the murder scenes like love scenes.
          </strong>
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <p>
         Below are additional selected citations in chronological order.
        </p>
        <p>

        </p>

         </strong>

        </p>

         In 1983 Claudia Tate edited and released a collection of interviews titled “Black Women Writers At Work”. Tate asked Maya Angelou about her manifold resourcefulness

          <strong>
           I believe talent is like electricity. We don’t understand electricity. We use it. Electricity makes no judgment.
          </strong>

         <strong>
          Dear Quote Investigator:
         </strong>
         The ability to perceive others as they see themselves is an enormously helpful guide for smooth and productive interactions. Here is a pertinent adage:
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          Tact is the ability to describe others as they see themselves.

         </strong>

        </p>

          QI
         </strong>

          <strong>
           Tact is the ability to describe others as they see themselves,
          </strong>
          says the Tulsa University Collegian.
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <p>
         “The Collegian” was (and remains) the newspaper of the University of Tulsa in Oklahoma. No attribution was provided. Thus, based on current information the creator was anonymous.

          What’s Not Going To Change in the Next 10 Years?
         </a>
        </h2>
       </header>
       <!-- .entry-header -->
       <div class="entry-content">
        <p>
         <strong>
          Jeff Bezos? Wolfgang R. Schmitt? Apocryphal?
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-439295" height="216" loading="lazy" sizes="(max-width: 540px) 100vw, 540px" src="https://quoteinvestigator.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/treetop07.jpg" srcset="https://quoteinvestigator.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/treetop07.jpg 540w, https://quoteinvestigator.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/treetop07-300x120.jpg 300w" width="540"/>
         <strong>
          Dear Quote Investigator:
         </strong>
         Business leaders in technology-based companies are often asked for ten year predictions of change. Apparently, Jeff Bezos, CEO of Amazon, once responded by spinning the inquiry. He said there was a more important question:
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          What’s not going to change in the next 10 years?
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <p>
         He argued that the answer was crucial because a company foundation must be built upon things that do not change. Would you please explore this topic?
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Quote Investigator:
         </strong>
         In October 2007 “Harvard Business Review” published an interview with Jeff Bezos. He stated that expanding into new areas was vital, but a nascent enterprise required five to seven years of nurturing before it could make a meaningful contribution to company economics. He was asked how he maintained confidence that such an investment would pay off:
         <a class="simple-footnote" href="#note-439293-1" id="return-note-439293-1" title="2007 October, Harvard Business Review (HBR), Article: The Institutional Yes, (Interview of Jeff Bezos conducted by HBR editors Julia Kirby and Thomas A. Stewart; interview was published in the magazine and on the website), Description: Magazine and website about management published by Harvard University of Massachusetts. (Accessed hbr.org March 3, 2021) link">
          <sup>
           1
          </sup>
         </a>
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          It helps to base your strategy on things that won’t change. When I’m talking with people outside the company, there’s a question that comes up very commonly:
          <strong>
           “What’s going to change in the next five to ten years?” But I very rarely get asked “What’s not going to change in the next five to ten years?”
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          At Amazon we’re always trying to figure that out, because you can really spin up flywheels around those things. All the energy you invest in them today will still be paying you dividends ten years from now. Whereas if you base your strategy first and foremost on more transitory things—who your competitors are, what kind of technologies are available, and so on—those things are going to change so rapidly that you’re going to have to change your strategy very rapidly, too.
         </p>
        </blockquote>

         </strong>

           Find the good. It’s all around you. Find it, showcase it and you’ll start believing in it.
          </strong>
          And so will most of the people who come into contact with you.
         </p>
         <p>
          Showcase the good.
          <br/>
          Believe in it.
          <br/>
          It’s real, baby.

       <header class="entry-header">
        <h2 class="entry-title">
         <a href="https://quoteinvestigator.com/2021/02/24/rascal/" rel="bookmark">
          He Is a Great Rascal. Ah! But He Is Our Rascal
         </a>
        </h2>
       </header>
       <!-- .entry-header -->
       <div class="entry-content">
        <p>
         <strong>
          Franklin D. Roosevelt? Abraham Lincoln? Thaddeus Stevens? Benjamin Butler? Philip Cook? Bill Higgins? John Franklin Carter? Justin Herman? Wayne Hays? Alistair Cooke? Cordell Hull? Anonymous? Apocryphal?
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Dear Quote Investigator:
         </strong>
         A participant in the harsh domain of political power often faces difficult decisions. For example, should one promote a member of one’s party even when one knows that the individual is a scoundrel? Also, should one maintain support for an ally even when the ally is disreputable or barbarous? The following dialog depicts a challenge and response:
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          “How can you support that scoundrel?”
          <br/>
          “He may be a scoundrel, but he’s our scoundrel.”
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <p>
         Over the years many other words have been used to describe the miscreant, e.g., rascal, scalawag, scoundrel, so-and-so, son-of-a-bitch, and bastard. Would you please explore this topic?
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Quote Investigator:
         </strong>
         The earliest match known to
         <strong>
          QI
         </strong>
         appeared in a Wilmington, North Carolina newspaper editorial in 1868. The two participants in the dialog were not identified. Boldface added to excerpts by
         <strong>
          QI
         </strong>
         :
         <a class="simple-footnote" href="#note-439265-1" id="return-note-439265-1" title="1868 July 26, The Daily Journal, “Our Rebels”, Quote Page 2, Column 1, Wilmington, North Carolina. (GenealogyBank)">
          <sup>
           1
          </sup>
         </a>
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          We are forcibly reminded by these arguments of the Radicals of the reply of one of their party, in attempting to persuade a rather conscientious member to vote for a certain candidate whose character was none the best.
          <strong>
           “He is a great rascal,”
          </strong>
          indignantly proposed the friend.
          <strong>
           “Ah! but he is our rascal,”
          </strong>
          was the significant rejoinder.
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <p>
         Many instances conforming to this template have appeared during the ensuing decades. Here is a sampling showing the key line together with a year:
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          1868:
         </strong>
         Ah! but he is our rascal.
         <br/>
         <strong>
          1875:
         </strong>
         Of course, of course, but which of ’em is our damned rascal?
         <br/>
         <strong>
          1889:
         </strong>
         Yes, I know, but then he’s our scalawag.
         <br/>
         <strong>
          1895:
         </strong>
         Never mind that; all we want to know is that he is our scoundrel.
         <br/>
         <strong>
          1904:
         </strong>
         Yes, I know, but he is our scoundrel.
         <br/>
         <strong>
          1934:
         </strong>
         After all, Blank isn’t so bad. He’s our So and So!
         <br/>
         <strong>
          1934:
         </strong>
         After all, Blank isn’t so bad. He’s our son-of-a-bitch!
         <br/>
         <strong>
          1948:
         </strong>
         He’s a sonofabitch but he’s ours.
         <br/>
         <strong>
          1962:
         </strong>
         He may be a son of a bitch, but he’s our son of a bitch.
         <br/>
         <strong>
          1969:
         </strong>
         He was a Grade-A bastard, but at least he was our bastard and not theirs.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          QI
         </strong>
         wishes to acknowledge researchers Bonnie Taylor-Blake and Barry Popik who identified many valuable examples.
        </p>
        <p>
         Below are additional selected citations in chronological order.

          One Must Have a Heart of Stone To Read the Death of Little Nell Without Laughing
         </a>
        </h2>
       </header>
       <!-- .entry-header -->
       <div class="entry-content">
        <p>
         <strong>
          Oscar Wilde? Ada Leverson? Hesketh Pearson? Leslie Stokes? Sewell Stokes? Apocryphal?
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-439262" height="283" loading="lazy" sizes="(max-width: 540px) 100vw, 540px" src="https://quoteinvestigator.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/02/littlenell09.jpg" srcset="https://quoteinvestigator.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/02/littlenell09.jpg 540w, https://quoteinvestigator.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/02/littlenell09-300x157.jpg 300w" width="540"/>
         <strong>
          Dear Quote Investigator:
         </strong>
         Charles Dickens published “The Old Curiosity Shop” in 1841. Nell Trent (Little Nell) was the virtuous child protagonist of the tale. The book was extremely popular, and most contemporary readers were saddened when they learned of Nell’s demise. Yet, some critics have viewed Dicken’s book as overly sentimental and emotionally manipulative. Here are two versions of a paraprosdokian:
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          One must have a heart of stone to read the death of Little Nell without laughing.
         </p>
         <p>
          One would have to have a heart of stone to read the death of little Nell without dissolving into tears … of laughter.
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <p>
         This remark has been attributed to the famous wit Oscar Wilde, but I have been unable to find a solid citation. Would you please explore this topic?
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Quote Investigator:
         </strong>
         Oscar Wilde died in 1900. The two earliest citations known to
         <strong>
          QI
         </strong>
         appeared three decades later.
        </p>
        <p>
         The biographer Hesketh Pearson wrote the introduction to a collection of Oscar Wilde’s works published in 1930 within the “Everyman’s Library” series. Pearson described the successes of Wilde’s comedies in the 1890’s, and he suggested that the playwright spoke the line during that period. Yet, Pearson did not explain how he learned about the witticism. Boldface added to excerpts by
         <strong>
          QI
         </strong>
         :
         <a class="simple-footnote" href="#note-439260-1" id="return-note-439260-1" title="1950 (First published in 1930), Plays, Prose Writings, And Poems by Oscar Wilde, Introduction by Hesketh Pearson, Series: Number 858 of Everyman’s Library, Section: Introduction, Quote Page xiii, Publisher: J. M. Dent &amp; Sons, London. (Verified with scans)">
          <sup>
           1
          </sup>
         </a>
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          It should be added that neither success nor misfortune could impair Wilde’s wit, the peculiar quality of which was exemplified at about this period in his comment on a scene by Dickens:
          <strong>
           “One must have a heart of stone to read the death of Little Nell without laughing.”
          </strong>
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <p>
         Also in 1930 author Ada Leverson, one of Wilde’s friends, published “Letters to the Sphinx from Oscar Wilde” which included her reminiscences about her relationship with Wilde.
         <a class="simple-footnote" href="#note-439260-2" id="return-note-439260-2" title="1930, Letters to the Sphinx from Oscar Wilde With Reminiscences of the Author by Ada Leverson by Oscar Wilde, Limited edition of 275 copies, Quote Page 42, Duckworth, London. (Not yet verified)">
          <sup>
           2
          </sup>
         </a>
         Excerpts from this book were reprinted in “The Sphinx and Her Circle: A Biographical Sketch of Ada Leverson, 1862-1933” by Violet Wyndham. The following 1930 text was reprinted in the 1963 book:
         <a class="simple-footnote" href="#note-439260-3" id="return-note-439260-3" title="1963, The Sphinx and Her Circle: A Biographical Sketch of Ada Leverson, 1862-1933 by Violet Wyndham, Reminiscences by Ada Leverson, 3: Afterwards, Quote Page 119, Vanguard Press, New York. (Verified with scans)">
          <sup>
           3
          </sup>
         </a>
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          He never liked even the grotesque part of Dickens. To those who praised Dickens, he said,
          <strong>
           ‘One must have a heart of stone to read the death of Little Nell without laughing’.
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          Of Max Beerbohm he said, ‘He plays with words as one plays with what one loves’. Adding, ‘When you are alone with him, Sphinx, does he take off his face and reveal his mask.”’
         </p>
        </blockquote>

         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           “You cannot define a person on just one thing,”
          </strong>
          she said.
          <strong>
           “You can’t just forget all these wonderful and good things that a person has done
