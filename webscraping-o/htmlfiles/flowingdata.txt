           Inadequate hate crime statistics
          </a>
         </h1>
        </div>
        <div class="clr">
        </div>
        <div class="byinfo two columns alpha">
         March 22, 2021
         <div style="margin-top:1.5rem">
          <h3 class="toplevel">
           Topic
          </h3>
          <strong>
           <a href="https://flowingdata.com/category/statistics/" rel="category tag">
            Statistics
           </a>
          </strong>
          /
          <a href="https://flowingdata.com/tag/crime/" rel="tag">
           crime
          </a>
          ,
          <a href="https://flowingdata.com/tag/hate/" rel="tag">

          </a>
          ,
          <a href="https://flowingdata.com/tag/police/" rel="tag">
           police
          </a>
          ,
          <a href="https://flowingdata.com/tag/propublica/" rel="tag">
           ProPublica
          </a>
         </div>
        </div>
        <div class="nine columns omega" id="entry-content-wrapper">
         <div class="entry">
          <p>
           For ProPublica, Ken Schwencke reports on
           <a href="https://www.propublica.org/article/why-america-fails-at-gathering-hate-crime-statistics?token=EzIEhjuqzmfWpMtLauWzrnGrMwnBSrzo">
            a poor data system that relies on local law enforcement
           </a>
           to voluntarily enter data:
          </p>
          <blockquote>
           <p>
            Local law enforcement agencies reported a total of 6,121 hate crimes in 2016 to the FBI, but estimates from the National Crime Victimization Survey, conducted by the federal government, pin the number of potential hate crimes at almost 250,000 a year — one indication of the inadequacy of the FBI’s data.
           </p>
           <p>
            “The current statistics are a complete and utter joke,” said Roy Austin, former deputy assistant attorney general in the Department of Justice’s civil rights division. Austin also worked at the White House on data and civil rights and helped develop an open data plan for police data.
           </p>
          </blockquote>
          <p>
           Garbage in, garbage out.
          </p>
         </div>

           In what seems to have become a trend of making more and more detailed election maps, NYT’s The Upshot
           <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2021/03/17/upshot/partisan-segregation-maps.html">
            mapped results down to the addresses of 180 million voters
           </a>
           :
          </p>
          <blockquote>
           <p>
            The maps above — and throughout this article — show their estimates of partisanship down to the individual voter, colored by the researchers’ best guess based on public data like demographic information, voter registration and whether voters participated in party primaries.
           </p>
           <p>
            We can’t know how any individual actually voted. But these maps show how Democrats and Republicans can live in very different places, even within the same city, in ways that go beyond the urban-suburban-rural patterns visible in aggregated election results.
           </p>
          </blockquote>

          </a>
         </h1>
        </div>
        <div class="clr">
        </div>
        <div class="byinfo two columns alpha">
         March 9, 2021
         <div style="margin-top:1.5rem">
          <h3 class="toplevel">
           Topic
          </h3>
          <strong>
           <a href="https://flowingdata.com/category/statistics/" rel="category tag">
            Statistics
           </a>
          </strong>
          /
          <a href="https://flowingdata.com/tag/fiction/" rel="tag">
           fiction
          </a>
          ,
          <a href="https://flowingdata.com/tag/machine-learning/" rel="tag">
           machine learning
          </a>
          ,
          <a href="https://flowingdata.com/tag/quartz/" rel="tag">
           Quartz
          </a>
          ,
          <a href="https://flowingdata.com/tag/wattpad/" rel="tag">
           Wattpad
          </a>
         </div>

        <div class="nine columns omega" id="entry-content-wrapper">
         <div class="entry">
          <p>
           <a href="https://flowingdata.com/2021/03/09/a-i-love-story/">
            Speaking of
           </a>
           A.I. and fiction, Adam Epstein for Quartz reported on how Wattpad, the platform for people to share stories,
           <a href="https://qz.com/1963544/is-wattpad-and-its-machine-learning-tool-the-future-of-tv/">
            uses machine learning to find potential movies
           </a>
           :
          </p>
          <blockquote>
           <p>
            Wattpad uses a machine-learning program called StoryDNA to scan all the stories on its platform and surface the ones that seem like candidates for TV or film development. It works on both macro and micro levels, analyzing big-picture audience engagement trends to identify the genres picking up steam, while also looking at the specific stories that got popular quickly and calculating what made them so appealing.
           </p>
           <p>
            The tool can break stories down to their vocabularies and sentence structures (a story’s “DNA,” if you will) and then compare those to other stories to deduce what really makes a work of fiction popular. It also looks at how often users comment on stories and, when they do, what exactly they’re saying. Its goal is to examine all these clues to uncover the precise combination of story elements—genre, emotion, grammar, the list goes on—that hooks audiences to the point they’ll follow its journey onto a visual medium.
           </p>
          </blockquote>
          <p>
           Maybe I’m just getting old, but this sounds terrible.
          </p>

         <h1>
          <a href="https://flowingdata.com/2021/03/09/10-statistical-lessons-from-the-past-pandemic-year/" rel="bookmark">
           10 statistical lessons from the past pandemic year
          </a>
         </h1>
        </div>
        <div class="clr">
        </div>
        <div class="byinfo two columns alpha">
         March 9, 2021
         <div style="margin-top:1.5rem">
          <h3 class="toplevel">
           Topic
          </h3>
          <strong>
           <a href="https://flowingdata.com/category/statistics/" rel="category tag">
            Statistics
           </a>
          </strong>
          /
          <a href="https://flowingdata.com/tag/coronavirus/" rel="tag">
           coronavirus
          </a>
          ,
          <a href="https://flowingdata.com/tag/learning/" rel="tag">
           learning
          </a>
          ,
          <a href="https://flowingdata.com/tag/royal-statistical-society/" rel="tag">
           Royal Statistical Society
          </a>
         </div>
        </div>
        <div class="nine columns omega" id="entry-content-wrapper">
         <div class="entry">
          <p>
           The Royal Statistical Society published
           <a href="https://rss.org.uk/policy-campaigns/policy/covid-19-task-force/statistics,-data-and-covid/">
            ten lessons governments should takeaway from this year
           </a>
           , which should naturally apply to standard data practice:
          </p>
          <blockquote>
           <ol role="list" start="1">
            <li>
             <strong>
              Invest in public health data
             </strong>
             – which should be regarded as critical national infrastructure and a full review of health data should be conducted
            </li>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Publish evidence
             </strong>
             – all evidence considered by governments and their advisers must be published in a timely and accessible manner
            </li>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Be clear and open about data
             </strong>
             – government should invest in a central portal, from which the different sources of official data, analysis protocols and up-to-date results can be found
            </li>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Challenge the misuse of statistics
             </strong>
             – the Office for Statistics Regulation should have its funding augmented so it can better hold the government to account
            </li>
            <li>
             <strong>
              The media needs to step up its responsibilities
             </strong>
             – government should support media institutions that invest in specialist scientific and medical reporting
            </li>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Build decision makers’ statistical skills
             </strong>
             – politicians and senior officials should seek out statistical training
            </li>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Build an effective infectious disease surveillance system to monitor the spread of disease
             </strong>
             – the government should ensure that a real-time surveillance system is ready for future pandemics
            </li>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Increase scrutiny and openness for new diagnostic tests
             </strong>
             – similar steps to those adopted for vaccine and pharmaceutical evaluation should be followed for diagnostic tests
            </li>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Health data is incomplete without social care data
             </strong>
             – improving social care data should be a central part of any review of UK health data
            </li>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Evaluation should be put at the heart of policy
             </strong>
             – efficient evaluations or experiments should be incorporated into any intervention from the start.
            </li>
           </ol>
          </blockquote>
          <p>
           See the full report
           <a href="https://rss.org.uk/policy-campaigns/policy/covid-19-task-force/statistics,-data-and-covid/">
            here
           </a>
           .
          </p>
         </div>

         <h1>
          <a href="https://flowingdata.com/2021/03/08/forecasting-covid-19-cases-in-the-early-goings/" rel="bookmark">
           Forecasting Covid-19 cases in the early goings
          </a>
         </h1>
        </div>
        <div class="clr">
        </div>
        <div class="byinfo two columns alpha">
         March 8, 2021
         <div style="margin-top:1.5rem">
          <h3 class="toplevel">
           Topic
          </h3>
          <strong>
           <a href="https://flowingdata.com/category/statistics/" rel="category tag">
            Statistics
           </a>
          </strong>
          /
          <a href="https://flowingdata.com/tag/coronavirus/" rel="tag">
           coronavirus
          </a>
          ,
          <a href="https://flowingdata.com/tag/forecasting/" rel="tag">
           forecasting
          </a>
          ,
          <a href="https://flowingdata.com/tag/youyang-gu/" rel="tag">
           Youyang Gu
          </a>
         </div>
        </div>
        <div class="nine columns omega" id="entry-content-wrapper">
         <div class="entry">
          <p>
           There was a lot of uncertainty in the beginning of the pandemic, so the forecasts varied across sources. There were also many forecasts. Youyang Gu provided on of those forecasts, and it predicted well. Ashlee Vance reporting for Bloomberg on
           <a href="https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2021-02-19/covid-pandemic-how-youyang-gu-used-ai-and-data-to-make-most-accurate-prediction">
            the Covid-19 forecasting work of Youyang Gu
           </a>
           :
          </p>
          <blockquote>
           <p>
            The novel, sophisticated twist of Gu’s model came from his use of machine learning algorithms to hone his figures. After MIT, Gu spent a couple years working in the financial industry writing algorithms for high-frequency trading systems in which his forecasts had to be accurate if he wanted to keep his job. When it came to Covid, Gu kept comparing his predictions to the eventual reported death totals and constantly tuned his machine learning software so that it would lead to ever more precise prognostications. Even though the work required the same hours as a demanding full-time job, Gu volunteered his time and lived off his savings. He wanted his data to be seen as free of any conflicts of interest or political bias.
           </p>
          </blockquote>
          <p>
           Reading this, it felt a little bit like cherry-picking the forecast that was best, but I don’t know enough to decide. It does seem to highlight though some of the limitations of larger organizations that don’t always have the best point of view.
          </p>
         </div>
