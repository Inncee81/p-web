      <h2>
       <a href="https://www.thisiscolossal.com/2021/03/lola-dupre-collages/">
        New Perspective-Bending Collages by Lola Dupré Distort and Reconfigure Pets and Portraits
       </a>
      </h2>
      <div class="post_details">
       <h3 class="date">
        <a href="https://www.thisiscolossal.com/2021/03/lola-dupre-collages/">
         March 19, 2021
        </a>
       </h3>
       <h3 class="author">
        <a href="https://www.thisiscolossal.com/author/gebert/">

        </a>
       </h3>
       <div class="clear">
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="wp-caption alignnone" id="attachment_142545" style="width: 1825px">
       <img alt="" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-142545" class="wp-image-142545 size-full" height="2560" loading="lazy" sizes="(max-width: 1815px) 100vw, 1815px" src="https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/dupre-2-scaled.jpg" srcset="https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/dupre-2-scaled.jpg 1815w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/dupre-2-640x903.jpg 640w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/dupre-2-960x1354.jpg 960w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/dupre-2-1089x1536.jpg 1089w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/dupre-2-1452x2048.jpg 1452w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/dupre-2-624x880.jpg 624w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/dupre-2-640x903@2x.jpg 1280w" width="1815"/>
       <p class="wp-caption-text" id="caption-attachment-142545">
        “Cleo” (2020), 8.2 x 11.6 inches. All images © Lola Dupré, shared with permission
       </p>
      </div>
      <p>
       Glasgow-based artist
       <a href="https://loladupre.com/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">
        Lola Dupré
       </a>
       (
       <a href="https://www.thisiscolossal.com/?s=Lola+Dupre">
        previously
       </a>
       ) continues her practice of slicing and rearranging photographs and art historical works into cleverly surreal collages. Her newest manipulations include a blockheaded Léon Bonnat, an entire row of irresistible puppy eyes, and a twisted rendition of George Stubbs’s “
       <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Kongouro_from_New_Holland" rel="noopener" target="_blank">
        The Kongouro from New Holland
       </a>
       .” Dupré’s cat, Charlie, still finds himself as fodder for the unusual works—see two pieces centered on him below—and the artist is currently in the process of creating her 33rd portrait of the orange-and-white feline. Find more of the Dupré’s compositions in the latest issue of
       <a href="https://standartmag.com/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">
        <em>
         Standart Magazine
        </em>
       </a>
       , shop originals and prints on
       <a href="https://loladupre.com/shop" rel="noopener" target="_blank">
        her site
       </a>
       , and see the distorted works in person at Portland’s
       <a href="https://brassworksgallery.com/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">
        Brassworks Gallery

      <p>
       A moth-human hybrid, striped coral, and a smoking frog sporting a tracksuit inhabit
       <a href="https://www.catjohnston.com/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">
        Cat Johnston
       </a>
       ’s fantastical ecosystem crafted from paper and textiles. The playfully bizarre creatures are inspired by monsters, mythology, and folklore, evoking deities and magnifying the strange qualities of plants and animals. Johnston created many of the lifeforms shown here shortly after moving to San Francisco and exploring the environment. “I was blown away by all the strange and lovely cacti and succulents and the animals I saw there (hummingbirds and pelicans and raccoons!) and wanted to create a landscape of plants and creatures that felt as alien and magical as California did to me,” she says.
      </p>
      <p>
       Currently living and working in Portland, the illustrator and model maker first worked with paper for a stop-motion animation she did with Andersen M and Tundra studios and has been creating with the material since. She’s in the process of crafting two more characters, which she’ll be sharing soon on
       <a href="https://www.instagram.com/catjohnstonnn/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">
        Instagram
       </a>
       . You also might enjoy
       <a href="https://www.thisiscolossal.com/?s=Roberto+Benavidez">
        Roberto Benavidez’s piñatas
       </a>
       and
       <a href="https://www.thisiscolossal.com/?s=Cat+Rabbit">
        the felt storybook creatures
       </a>
       by Cat Rabbit.
      </p>

        “You Better Be Good” (2021), oil on panel, 36 x 36 centimeters. All images © Rosso Emerald Crimson, shared with permission
       </p>
      </div>
      <p>
       In her exquisite portraiture, London-based artist
       <a href="https://www.rossoart.net/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">
        Rosso Emerald Crimson
       </a>
       renders female subjects who emerge through a haze of pastels and muted tones. She infuses the dreamy oil paintings with responses to current affairs and questions about the future, which often serve as a catalyst for her projects. “I don’t ‘think’ specifically about political or ethical issues when I paint although my creative flow is undoubtedly fuelled by the impressions and emotions many global events leave subconsciously,” she tells Colossal. Issues of racial justice and the unrealistic portrayal of beauty have both played a role in her recent works, including the compelling portrait of a young Black girl titled “What Are We Waiting For.”
      </p>
      <p>
       Generally, the subjects are people Rosso has a relationship with or someone who’s caught her eye, although she’s expanded her purview to models she’s never met as a way to adapt to pandemic restrictions. The artist often depicts the women and girls staring forward with unsmiling expressions. “I am enchanted by the diversity of human beings which is what truly makes us beautiful,” she says.
      </p>
      <p>
       If you’re in London, you can see Rosso’s paintings that are part of an exhibition celebrating Women’s History Month at
       <a href="https://zebraonegallery.com/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">
        Zebra One Gallery
       </a>
       until March 31. She’ll also have pieces on view at
       <a href="https://www.southbankcentre.co.uk/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">
        Southbank Centre
       </a>
       this summer and a solo show at
       <a href="https://www.chrom.art/chrom-gallery/">
        Chrom Art Gallery
       </a>
       in November. Prints and originals are available in
       <a href="https://www.rossoart.net/shop1" rel="noopener" target="_blank">
        her shop
       </a>
       , and you can see works-in-progress on
       <a href="https://www.instagram.com/rossoartist/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">
        Instagram
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <a href="https://www.thisiscolossal.com/2021/03/stacey-lee-webber-money/">
        Subversively Embroidered Money and Penny Sculptures Question Historical Narratives
       </a>
      </h2>
      <div class="post_details">
       <h3 class="date">
        <a href="https://www.thisiscolossal.com/2021/03/stacey-lee-webber-money/">
         March 18, 2021
        </a>
       </h3>
       <h3 class="author">
        <a href="https://www.thisiscolossal.com/author/gebert/">
         Grace Ebert
        </a>
       </h3>
       <div class="clear">
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="wp-caption alignnone" id="attachment_142489" style="width: 2058px">
       <img alt="" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-142489" class="wp-image-142489 size-full" height="2048" loading="lazy" sizes="(max-width: 2048px) 100vw, 2048px" src="https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/webber-7.jpeg" srcset="https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/webber-7.jpeg 2048w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/webber-7-640x640.jpeg 640w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/webber-7-960x960.jpeg 960w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/webber-7-150x150.jpeg 150w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/webber-7-1536x1536.jpeg 1536w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/webber-7-624x624.jpeg 624w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/webber-7-50x50.jpeg 50w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/webber-7-640x640@2x.jpeg 1280w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/webber-7-960x960@2x.jpeg 1920w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/webber-7-150x150@2x.jpeg 300w" width="2048"/>
       <p class="wp-caption-text" id="caption-attachment-142489">
        From Insurrection Bills. All images © Stacey Lee Webber, shared with permission
       </p>
      </div>
      <p>
       Throughout 2020,
       <a href="https://www.staceyleewebber.com/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">
        Stacey Lee Webber
       </a>
       developed
       <a href="https://www.staceyleewebber.com/insurrection-bills" rel="noopener" target="_blank">
        <em>
         Insurrection Bills
        </em>
       </a>
       , a revisionary collection of United States currency overlaid with subversive stitches: flames envelop monuments, a wall is left unfinished, and an eclectic array of face masks disguise Abraham Lincoln’s portrait. Contrasting the muted tones of the paper, the vibrant embroideries stand in stark contrast and as amended narratives to those depicted on the various denominations. “The series references feelings of anger, turmoil, and frustration during the tense political climate while recontextualizing and questioning the beloved iconography we see on our money,” she tells Colossal.
      </p>
      <p>
       Currently working from her studio and home in Philadelphia’s
       <a href="https://www.globedyeworks.com/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">
        Globe Dye Works
       </a>
       , Webber is formally trained in metalsmithing—she has an MFA from the University of Wisconsin, where she initially began using currency as the basis of her projects—and sees the two mediums as an ongoing conversation. Embroidery “allows me to work in a quieter setting outside of my metal shop acting as a sort of ying to the yang, soft and hard, masculine and feminine,” she says.
      </p>
      <p>
       Many of Webber’s sculptures involve soldering coins, including the copper penny works that make up
       <em>
        The Craftsmen Series
       </em>
       and question the value of blue-collar labor in the U.S. Comprised of hollow, life-sized tools, the collection visualizes “putting endless amounts of work into a single cent,” the artist says.
      </p>
      <p>
       Webber has multiple exhibitions this year, including at
       <a href="https://www.tw-fineart.com" rel="noopener" target="_blank">
        TW Fine Art Palm Beach Outpost
       </a>
       in April, Philadelphia’s
       <a href="https://www.bertrandproductions.com" rel="noopener" target="_blank">
        Bertrand Productions
       </a>
       in October, and
       <a href="https://thepaperfair.com/ny" rel="noopener" target="_blank">
        Art on Paper Fair
       </a>
       in New York City this November. If you can’t see the currency-based projects in person, head to
       <a href="https://www.instagram.com/staceyleewebber/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">
        Instagram,
       </a>
       where the artist shares a larger collection of her works and glimpses into her studio.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <a href="https://www.thisiscolossal.com/2021/03/gerard-mas-sculpture/">
        Busts of Unabashed Women by Gerard Mas Are Sculpted with a Contemporary and Cheeky Twist
       </a>
      </h2>
      <div class="post_details">
       <h3 class="date">
        <a href="https://www.thisiscolossal.com/2021/03/gerard-mas-sculpture/">
         March 18, 2021
        </a>
       </h3>
       <h3 class="author">
        <a href="https://www.thisiscolossal.com/author/gebert/">
         Grace Ebert
        </a>
       </h3>
       <div class="clear">
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="wp-caption alignnone" id="attachment_142467" style="width: 2010px">
       <img alt="" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-142467" class="wp-image-142467 size-full" height="2429" loading="lazy" sizes="(max-width: 2000px) 100vw, 2000px" src="https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/mas-3.jpg" srcset="https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/mas-3.jpg 2000w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/mas-3-640x777.jpg 640w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/mas-3-960x1166.jpg 960w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/mas-3-1265x1536.jpg 1265w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/mas-3-1686x2048.jpg 1686w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/mas-3-624x758.jpg 624w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/mas-3-640x777@2x.jpg 1280w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/mas-3-960x1166@2x.jpg 1920w" width="2000"/>
       <p class="wp-caption-text" id="caption-attachment-142467">
        “Lady of the chewing gum,” polychrome resin. All images © Gerard Mas, shared with permission
       </p>
      </div>
      <p>
       Despite their modest clothing and perfectly plaited hair, the women that artist
       <a href="http://www.gerardmas.com/esp-obra.htm" rel="noopener" target="_blank">
        Gerard Mas
       </a>
       sculpts are spirited, brazen, and undeniably shameless. Whether blowing a wad of bubblegum, sporting visible tan lines, or unabashedly digging in their noses, the corset-clad figures are steeped in humor and wit and cast a contemporary light on the long-held conventions of the medium.
      </p>
      <p>
       Mas began the ongoing series a few years ago as he ventured into figurative sculpture and struggled with portraying perfection and beauty. He shares:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        This was an impossible job. There was always something that broke that beauty. And a sculpture attempting to speak of beauty with some disproportion or flagrant compositional flaw is pretentious if not ridiculous… I decided to anticipate that failure and deliberately introduce discordant elements that broke that pretended beauty by making our sense of good taste squeak. Let’s say it’s an ode to the impossibility of beauty.
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       Based near Barcelona, Mas originally trained as a restorer with a focus on reconstructing floral ornaments in architecture. “In my obsession with contemplating the art of other times, I also realized that our current cultural codes prevent us from contemplating the art of the past without reinventing its meaning. We are subjected to an avalanche of daily images that shapes the way we look,” he says. This experience continues to inform his practice that seamlessly melds traditional techniques—his use of standard materials like marble, alabaster, carved wood, gilding, and polychrome, for example—and contemporary subject matter.
      </p>
      <p>
       If you’re in Madrid, you can see Mas’s sculptures at
       <a href="https://www.ifema.es/en/estampa" rel="noopener" target="_blank">
        Estampa
       </a>
       from April 8 to 11. Otherwise, peruse a larger collection of his figurative works on
       <a href="http://www.gerardmas.com/esp-obra.htm" rel="noopener" target="_blank">
        his site
       </a>
       and
       <a href="https://www.instagram.com/gerard_mas/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">
        Instagram
       </a>
       . (via
       <a href="https://www.thejealouscurator.com/blog/2021/02/24/gerard-mas/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">
        The Jealous Curator
       </a>
       )
      </p>

      <p>
       New photographs by
       <a href="http://www.shainblumphoto.com/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">
        Michael Shainblum
       </a>
       (
       <a href="https://www.thisiscolossal.com/?s=Michael+Shainblum">
        previously
       </a>
       ) capitalize on the grandeur of Yosemite National Park and cast it in an ethereal light. Shot in winter just after a dusting of snow, the series is serene and dream-like and spotlights the details that sometimes are lost in the vast wilderness: rainbow mist envelops a waterfall, dense fog hangs among a mountain top, and the warm glow of golden hour radiates across a rocky ridge.
      </p>
