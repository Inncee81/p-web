         <strong>

         </strong>
        </p>
        <div class="center-img">
         <a aria-label="open enlarged image" href="image.php?image=https://static.tweaktown.com/news/7/8/78328_28_remedy-making-games-for-two-different-console-generations-sucks_full.png" target="_blank">

         </a>
        </div>
        <p>
         Backwards compatibility is the
         <a href="https://www.tweaktown.com/reviews/9742/xbox-series-dream-machine/index.html">
          <strong>
           single most important feature for next-gen hardware
          </strong>
         </a>
         and it's great for gamers. But Microsoft's and Sony's insistence on last-gen support is giving developers headaches. Hardware supply limitations are pressuring devs to make their games playable on both the 2013 and 2020 console gens--it's either that or miss out on big sales numbers because there's a lot more last-gen hardware than there is next-gen. Right now, devs have to make their games work on
         <em>
          eight separate consoles
         </em>
         :
        </p>
        <ol>
         <li>
          <span>
           PS4
          </span>
         </li>
         <li>
          <span>

          </span>

         <li>
          <span>

          </span>
         </li>
         <li>
          <span>
           Xbox One
          </span>
         </li>
         <li>
          <span>
           Xbox One S
          </span>
         </li>
         <li>
          <span>
           Xbox One X
          </span>
         </li>
         <li>
          <span>
           Xbox Series S
          </span>
         </li>
         <li>
          <span>
           Xbox Series X
          </span>
         </li>
        </ol>
        <p>

Control, for example, had to be entirely re-worked to run on next-gen hardware. It's a pain point that Remedy had to deal with--imagine how Microsoft's developers feel knowing the massive variance in Xbox console hardware power.
