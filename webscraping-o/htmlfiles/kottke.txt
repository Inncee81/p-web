   <h2>
    <a href="https://kottke.org/21/03/how-to-safely-remove-bees-for-relocation">
     How to Safely Remove Bees for Relocation
    </a>
   </h2>
   <div class="meta">
    <div class="author">
     <span class="pre-name">
      posted by
     </span>
     <a href="http://www.kottke.org">
      Jason Kottke
     </a>
     <span class="timestamp">
      <i aria-hidden="true" class="fa fa-clock-o" style="font-weight:300;">
      </i>
      Mar 19, 2021
     </span>
    </div>
   </div>
   <p class="widescreen embedded">
    <iframe allow="accelerometer; autoplay; clipboard-write; encrypted-media; gyroscope; picture-in-picture" allowfullscreen="" data-src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/a_VBqXouGgE" frameborder="0" src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODdhAQABAPAAAP///wAAACwAAAAAAQABAEACAkQBADs=" title="YouTube video player" width="100%">
    </iframe>
   </p>
   <div class="post">
    <p>
     This video of beekeeper Erika Thompson removing a hive of bees from the floor of an old shed was absolutely riveting! I loved every minute of it. Working without a beekeeping suit or even gloves, Thompson begins by locating the hive with a thermal imager, cuts open the floor, and gently lifts up the flooring to reveal the hive. After transferring the hive’s honeycomb to a new box hive, she cajoles the bees into their new home through the use of smoke, relocating the queen, and even scooping them in there with her bare hand. She ends the video with “and it was another great day of saving the bees” and that’s 100% right.
    </p>
    <p>
     You can watch more videos by Thompson
     <a href="https://www.youtube.com/c/TexasBeeworks/videos">
      on YouTube
     </a>
     , including
     <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=t6nHpoLESa8">
      this longer one of her removing bees from a camper
     </a>
     . You can also check out her work
     <a href="https://www.instagram.com/texasbeeworks/">
      on Instagram
     </a>
     or
     <a href="https://www.austinmonthly.com/a-day-in-the-life-of-an-austin-beekeeper/">
      read about how she started her company
     </a>
     ,
     <a href="https://texasbeeworks.com/">
      Texas Beeworks
     </a>
     .
    </p>
    <blockquote>
     <p>
      “I didn’t become a beekeeper because I wanted to sell honey, and I think that’s what separates me from a lot of other beekeepers,” she says. “Whatever way you’re inspired by bees or to keep bees I think is wonderful. But in full transparency … I’d rather focus on creating more bees than having them produce more honey.”
     </p>
    </blockquote>
    <p>
     Her videos have been going viral on TikTok lately, which led to
     <a href="https://www.today.com/tmrw/beekeeper-erika-thompson-scoops-bees-her-bare-hands-t212030">
      an appearance on the Today Show
     </a>
     , where she explained why she was working without protective gear.
    </p>
    <blockquote>
     <p>
      “Most honey bees are very gentle. They’re docile and they don’t want to sting you. I’ve been doing this for a long time and over the years I’ve learned to read the bees’ behavior and these were just very calm, gentle bees. They were also very cooperative and got into their new hive. This was just one case where I could work without gear and it was safer for me and the bees.”
     </p>
    </blockquote>
    <p>
     (via
     <a href="https://austinkleon.com/">
      austin kleon
     </a>
     )
    </p>

   <h2>
    <a href="https://kottke.org/21/03/the-invention-of-a-new-pasta-shape">
     The Invention of a New Pasta Shape
    </a>
   </h2>
   <div class="meta">
    <div class="author">
     <span class="pre-name">
      posted by
     </span>
     <a href="http://www.kottke.org">
      Jason Kottke
     </a>
     <span class="timestamp">
      <i aria-hidden="true" class="fa fa-clock-o" style="font-weight:300;">
      </i>
      Mar 19, 2021
     </span>
    </div>
   </div>
   <div class="post">
    <p>
     For the past three years, Dan Pashman of
     <a href="https://www.sporkful.com/">
      The Sporkful podcast
     </a>
     has been on something of a mission: to invent a new pasta shape. All of Pashman’s hard work has paid off with the debut of cascatelli pasta,
     <a href="https://www.sfoglini.com/products/sporkful">
      available for sale from Sfoglini
     </a>
     .
    </p>
    <p>
     <img alt="Cascatelli pasta" border="0" data-src="/plus/misc/images/cascatelli-pasta-01.jpg" height="1000" src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODdhAQABAPAAAP///wAAACwAAAAAAQABAEACAkQBADs=" width="1000"/>
    </p>
    <p>
     Pashman and Sfoglini engineered the new shape to maximize the amount of sauce that sticks to it, make it easier to get your fork on it, and have it feel good when you bite into it.
    </p>
    <p>
     <img alt="Cascatelli Pasta 02" border="0" data-src="/plus/misc/images/cascatelli-pasta-02.jpg" height="1000" src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODdhAQABAPAAAP///wAAACwAAAAAAQABAEACAkQBADs=" width="1000"/>
    </p>
    <blockquote>
     <p>
      Cascatelli is designed to maximize the three qualities by which Dan believes all pasta shapes should be judged:
     </p>
     <p>
      Sauceability: How readily sauce adheres to the shape
      <br/>
      Forkability: How easy it is to get the shape on your fork and keep it there
      <br/>
      Toothsinkability: How satisfying it is to sink your teeth into it
     </p>
    </blockquote>
    <p>
     Pashman documented the invention of cascatelli in a 5-part series on The Sporkful podcast —
     <a href="https://www.sporkful.com/mission-impastable-1-spaghetti-sucks/">
      you can listen to the first episode here
     </a>
     — and
     <a href="https://www.instagram.com/s/aGlnaGxpZ2h0OjE3ODYzOTA1ODY2NDgzOTY0?igshid=fvqxwa81z7nc&amp;story_media_id=2517176903157538890">
      on Instagram
     </a>
     . You can
     <a href="https://www.sfoglini.com/products/sporkful">
      order some cascatelli
     </a>
     to try it out at home, but it looks like they are currently sold out of everything aside from 5-lb bulk bags.
    </p>
    <p>
     See also
     <a href="https://kottke.org/18/03/how-to-make-29-different-shapes-of-pasta-by-hand">
      How to Make 29 Different Shapes of Pasta by Hand
     </a>
     and
     <a href="https://kottke.org/09/10/150-different-pasta-shapes">
      150 Different Pasta Shapes
     </a>
     .
    </p>

    <div class="author">
     <span class="pre-name">
      posted by
     </span>
     <a href="http://www.kottke.org">

     <span class="timestamp">
      <i aria-hidden="true" class="fa fa-clock-o" style="font-weight:300;">

     </span>
    </div>
   </div>
   <p class="widescreen embedded">
    <iframe allow="accelerometer; autoplay; clipboard-write; encrypted-media; gyroscope; picture-in-picture" allowfullscreen="" data-src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/ajpcHWBuV18" frameborder="0" src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODdhAQABAPAAAP///wAAACwAAAAAAQABAEACAkQBADs=" width="100%">
    </iframe>
   </p>
   <div class="post">
    <p>
     Back in December 2020, Nepal and China announced that the height of Mount Everest had been remeasured and
     <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2020/12/08/world/asia/mount-everest-china-nepal.html">
      updated
     </a>
     from a height of 8,848 meters (29,028.87 feet) to 8,848.86 meters (29,031.7 feet). Did the mountain get taller? Or the measuring more precise? And how do you measure the height of a mountain — or “sea level” for that matter — anyway?
    </p>
    <blockquote>
     <p>
      In December of 2020, China and Nepal made a joint announcement about a new measurement for Mount Everest: 8,849 meters. This is just the latest of several different surveys of Everest since the first measurement was taken in 1855. The reasons why the height has fluctuated have to do with surveying methodology, challenges in determining sea level, and the people who have historically been able to measure Everest.
     </p>
    </blockquote>
    <p>
     Also worth noting the (romanised) Nepalese and Tibetan names for the mountain: Sagarmāthā and Chomolungma.
     <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Everest#Name">
      The section on its name at Wikipedia
     </a>
     is pretty interesting — apparently George Everest, for whom the mountain was named, pronounced his name differently than we all do today.
    </p>

    <a href="https://kottke.org/21/03/how-sperm-whales-learned-to-collectively-outsmart-19th-century-whalers">
     How Sperm Whales Learned to Collectively Outsmart 19th-Century Whalers
    </a>
   </h2>
   <div class="meta">
    <div class="author">
     <span class="pre-name">
      posted by
     </span>
     <a href="http://www.kottke.org">
      Jason Kottke
     </a>
     <span class="timestamp">
      <i aria-hidden="true" class="fa fa-clock-o" style="font-weight:300;">
      </i>
      Mar 17, 2021
     </span>
    </div>
   </div>
   <div class="post">
    <p>
     <img alt="Sperm Whale" border="0" data-src="/plus/misc/images/sperm-whale.jpg" height="650" src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODdhAQABAPAAAP///wAAACwAAAAAAQABAEACAkQBADs=" width="1000"/>
    </p>
    <p>
     In the 19th century, when whaling fleets were first sent out to hunt sperm whales, the whales employed the same defensive strategy they used against orcas: forming the group into a tight circle. But what worked against orcas was not successful against whalers: the close grouping made the whales that much easier to kill.
     <a href="https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2021/mar/17/sperm-whales-in-19th-century-shared-ship-attack-information">
      According to a recent analysis of whalers’ logbooks
     </a>
     , within a few years the sperm whales collectively abandoned the grouping strategy in favor of swimming upwind from the sailing ships to escape.
    </p>
    <blockquote>
     <p>
      Sperm whales are highly socialised animals, able to communicate over great distances. They associate in clans defined by the dialect pattern of their sonar clicks. Their culture is matrilinear, and information about the new dangers may have been passed on in the same way whale matriarchs share knowledge about feeding grounds. Sperm whales also possess the largest brain on the planet. It is not hard to imagine that they understood what was happening to them.
     </p>
     <p>
      The hunters themselves realised the whales’ efforts to escape. They saw that the animals appeared to communicate the threat within their attacked groups. Abandoning their usual defensive formations, the whales swam upwind to escape the hunters’ ships, themselves wind-powered. ‘This was cultural evolution, much too fast for genetic evolution,’ says Whitehead.
     </p>
    </blockquote>
    <p>
     See also
     <a href="https://kottke.org/18/11/studying-humpback-whales-to-better-communicate-with-aliens">
      Studying Humpback Whales to Better Communicate with Aliens
     </a>
     .
    </p>

    <p>
     <img alt="Little Women tintype portrait" border="0" data-src="/plus/misc/images/little-women-tintype-01.jpg" height="1256" src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODdhAQABAPAAAP///wAAACwAAAAAAQABAEACAkQBADs=" width="1000"/>

    <p>
     Photographer
     <a href="https://www.wilsonwebb.com/">
      Wilson Webb
     </a>
     made
     <a href="https://www.instagram.com/p/B6CgQNiJGQ9/">
      these great tintype portraits
     </a>
     of the cast of Little Women. Invented in 1851,
     <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collodion_process">
      the collodion process
     </a>
     would have been in use during the time the movie takes place. You can read about Webb’s process
     <a href="https://petapixel.com/2020/02/12/behind-the-scenes-shooting-wet-plate-portraits-of-the-cast-of-little-women/">
      at PetaPixel
     </a>
     .
    </p>
    <blockquote>
     <p>
      To capture the actual portraits, Webb got his hands on a 130-year-old Dallmeyer lens that he strapped to a modern large format camera, and set up 25,000 Watt-seconds worth of flash to ensure he had enough light. That’s… a lot of light. So much that Webb says his subjects “can feel a wave of heat and they can also smell the ozone that’s created when the picture’s taken.”
     </p>
     <p>
      But despite all of this light — which allowed him to capture a much faster “shutter speed” than traditional wet plates — he still had the cast pose in a traditional fashion: facing the camera, stoic expression, sitting still for 30 seconds at a time to capture each individual frame.
     </p>
    </blockquote>
    <p>
     You can check out the whole series of portraits
     <a href="https://mymodernmet.com/wilson-webb-little-women-photographs/">
      at My Modern Met
     </a>
     .
    </p>
