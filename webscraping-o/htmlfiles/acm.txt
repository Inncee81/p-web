                 </h2>
                 <div class="dek">
                  <p>

The stories might range from your earliest introduction to computer science to overcoming a recent professional obstacle, and the technical women could vary from luminaries of the computing field to your high school CS teacher or current boss.

                  </a>
                 </h2>
                 <div class="dek">
                  <p>
                   ACM-W has announced Munmun De Choudhury, an Associate Professor at the School of Interactive Computing at Georgia Institute of Technology, as the winner of the 2021 ACM-W Rising Star Award. The award recognizes a woman whose early-career research has had a significant impact on the computing discipline. De Choudhury's research develops novel computational techniques, and technologies powered by them, to responsibly and ethically employ social media in quantifying, understanding, and improving personal and societal health and well-being.

                 <h2>
                  <a href="/articles/people-of-acm/2021/wil-van-der-aalst" target="_blank">
                   Meet Wil van der Aalst
                  </a>
                 </h2>
                 <div class="dek">
                  <p>
                   Wil van der Aalst is a Professor and head of the Process and Data Science (PADS) group at RWTH Aachen University in Aachen, Germany. He has often been called “the Godfather of Process Mining,” an emerging field that bridges the gap between traditional business management techniques and modern data science techniques. He is the author of the definitive textbook
                   <a href="https://www.springer.com/gp/book/9783662498507" target="_blank">
                    <em>
                     Process Mining
                    </em>
                   </a>
                   , as well as over 250 journal papers, 22 books (as author or editor), 550 refereed conference/workshop publications, and 80 book chapters. Van der Aalst is a Fellow of ACM, IEEE and IFIP.
                  </p>

                  <a href="/media-center/2020/july/ustpc-urges-narrower-definition-of-computer-fraud-abuse-act" target="_blank">
                   USTPC Urges Narrower Definition of Computer Fraud and Abuse Act
                  </a>
                 </h2>
                 <div class="dek">
                  <p>
                   ACM's US Technology Policy Committee filed a
                   <a href="/binaries/content/assets/public-policy/ustpc-amicus-brief-vanburen-v-us.pdf">
                    friend of the court brief
                   </a>
                   with the US Supreme Court in the landmark case of
                   <em>
                    Van Buren v. United States
                   </em>
                   —the first time it has reviewed the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act, a 1986 law that was originally intended to punish hacking. USTPC notes that the questions posed in this case have broad implications for data and computing scientists, as well as other professionals who use the internet and computing technology, particularly to access information posted online.
                  </p>

The statement calls on governments that adopt such systems to choose "only those which... respect and protect the rights of all individuals; safeguard personal data and privacy to the highest degree technically possible; and are subject to scrutiny by the scientific community and civil society before, during and after deployment."

                 <h2>
                  <a href="https://cacm.acm.org/magazines/2021/3/250710-the-decline-of-computers-as-a-general-purpose-technology/fulltext" target="_blank">
                   The Decline of Computers as a General Purpose Technology
                  </a>
                 </h2>
                 <div class="dek">
                  <p>
                   Neil Thompson and Svenja Spanuth argue that the unwinding of the economic cycle of the "general purpose technology" results in less marketing growth and slower technical progress. As CPU improvement slows, economic incentives push users toward specialized processors. This fragmentation means that parts of computing will progress at different rates. Read more in their contributed article, "Decline of Computers as a General Purpose Technology," in the March 2021 issue of
                   <i>
                    Communications of the ACM
                   </i>
                   .
                  </p>

                 <h2>
                  <a href="https://queue.acm.org/detail.cfm?id=3439229" target="_blank">
                   Differential Privacy: The Pursuit of Protections by Default
                  </a>
                 </h2>
                 <div class="dek">
                  <p>
                   As privacy violations have become rampant and calls for better measures to protect sensitive, personally identifiable information have primarily resulted in bureaucratic policies satisfying almost no one, differential privacy is emerging as a potential solution. In “Differential Privacy: The Pursuit of Protections by Default,” a Case Study in
                   <em>
                    ACM Queue
                   </em>
                   , Google’s Damien Desfontaines and Miguel Guevara reflect with Jim Waldo and Terry Coatta on the engineering challenges that lie ahead for differential privacy, as well as what remains to be done to achieve their ultimate goal of providing privacy protection by default.
                  </p>
                 </div>
                </div>
                <a href="https://queue.acm.org/detail.cfm?id=3439229" target="_blank">
