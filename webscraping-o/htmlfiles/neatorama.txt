            How Ida Holdgreve’s Stitches Helped the Wright Brothers Get Off the Ground
           </a>
          </h2>
         </header>

       <section class="postbody">
        <div class="postbody-text">
         <p>
          <noscript>
           <img alt="" data-height="428" data-width="600" height="428" src="https://uploads.neatorama.com/images/posts/352/118/118352/1616243481-0.jpg" width="600"/>
          </noscript>
          <img alt="" class="unveil" data-height="428" data-src="https://uploads.neatorama.com/images/posts/352/118/118352/1616243481-0.jpg" data-width="600" height="428" src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAAD/ACwAAAAAAQABAAACADs=" width="600"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          Ida Holdgreve answered an ad in a Dayton, Ohio, newspaper that said "plain sewing wanted." But it was a typo that should have said "plane" sewing. That's how the dressmaker ended up sewing airplane parts for the Wright Brothers.
         </p>
         <blockquote>
          <p>
           In 1910 and 1911, two odd buildings began to rise a mile-and-a-half west of the Wright brothers’ West Dayton home. Bowed parapets bookended the long one-story structures, their midsections arching like the crooks of serpents’ spines; wide windows reflected the pastoral world outside. This was the Wright Company factory, the first American airplane factory, and behind the buildings’ painted brick walls, Holdgreve sewed surfaces for some of the world’s first airplanes, making her a pioneer in the aviation industry.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           “As far as I know, she was the only woman who worked on the Wright Company factory floor,” says aviation writer Timothy R. Gaffney, author of The Dayton Flight Factory: The Wright Brothers &amp; The Birth of Aviation. “And she was earning her living making airplane parts. Since I haven’t found a woman working in this capacity any earlier, as far as I know, Ida Holdgreve was the first female American aerospace worker.”
          </p>
         </blockquote>
         <p>
          Holdgreve learned to build airplanes, stretching countless yards of muslin over their frames. As the industry grew, her expertise was recognized, and in World War I, Holgreve supervised a crew of seamstresses turning out more advanced airplanes for the war effort. Yet strangely, she never flew in an airplane herself until 1969!
          <a href="https://www.smithsonianmag.com/history/how-ida-holdgreves-stitches-helped-wright-brothers-get-off-ground-180977195/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
           Read the story of Ida Holgreve at Smithsonian.
          </a>
          <br/>
          <br/>
          (Image source: Wright State University Libraries' Special Collections &amp; Archives)
         </p>

         </header>
         <footer class="byline">

       <section class="postbody">
        <div class="postbody-text">
         <p>
          <noscript>
           <img alt="" data-height="595" data-width="600" height="595" src="https://uploads.neatorama.com/images/posts/350/118/118350/1616243258-0.jpg" width="600"/>
          </noscript>
          <img alt="" class="unveil" data-height="595" data-src="https://uploads.neatorama.com/images/posts/350/118/118350/1616243258-0.jpg" data-width="600" height="595" src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAAD/ACwAAAAAAQABAAACADs=" width="600"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          In the grand scheme of things, it hasn't been all that long that Western women were "allowed" to wear pants. It was only in 2013 that a French law forbidding women to wear trousers was repealed! (True, it hadn't been enforced for a long time.) A women wearing pants was scandalous until the 20th century, but the desire for practical clothing was always there. Lady Mary Wortley Montagu went to Constantinople in 1716 as her husband was the British ambassador, and was struck by the trousers worn by Turkish women.
         </p>
         <blockquote>
          <p>
           Lady Mary returned from her trip with trunks of clothing worn by the Muslim women she encountered, sharing them with members of her social circle, even posing for public portraits modelling the garments.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           She wrote about her experiences and observations, creating intrigue amongst the fashionable elite. Her letters and firsthand accounts invoked honest conversations about freedom of dress, property rights, and other social, economic, legal, and marital freedoms that women were denied in Europe. As more women began to travel and discovering foreign cultures, it seemed Western society, which held a strong Eurocentric bias, was falling behind the East regarding women’s rights issues.
          </p>
         </blockquote>
         <p>
          It was a couple of hundred years after Montague's travels before women in pants became common in the West.
          <a href="https://www.messynessychic.com/2021/03/17/the-not-so-straightforward-story-of-women-and-trousers/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">

       <section class="postbody">
        <div class="postbody-text">
         <p>
          <noscript>
           <img alt="" src="https://uploads.neatorama.com/images/posts/346/118/118346/The-Free-Tool-That-Can-Reveal-Who-Owns-A-Domain_0-x.jpg"/>
          </noscript>
          <img alt="" class="unveil" data-src="https://uploads.neatorama.com/images/posts/346/118/118346/The-Free-Tool-That-Can-Reveal-Who-Owns-A-Domain_0-x.jpg" src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAAD/ACwAAAAAAQABAAACADs="/>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="color: #000000;">
           Meet the specialized tool that can let you know who owns a website, without spending a long time looking through Google search results! The
          </span>
          <a href="https://lookup.icann.org/" rel="nofollow" style="color: #1155cc;" target="_blank">
           WhoIs lookup
          </a>
          <span style="color: #000000;">
           tool returns whatever available information there is about “who is” responsible for the site, regardless of how old the website is. There is certain private information that would not appear in the results for every site.
          </span>
          <a href="https://www.popsci.com/story/diy/whois-lookup-explained/" rel="nofollow" style="color: #1155cc;" target="_blank">
           Popular Science
          </a>
          <span style="color: #000000;">
           lists information people will find when running the tool:
          </span>
         </p>
         <ul>
          <li>
           Name: This is simply the domain name for the website you looked up.
          </li>
          <li>
           Internationalized domain name: If the domain is linked to a location that doesn’t use the
           <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_script" rel="nofollow" style="color: #1155cc;" target="_blank">
            Latin characters
           </a>
           present in English and other European languages, you may see its name displayed here in Arabic, Greek, or another type of script.
          </li>
          <li>
           Registry domain ID: This is the unique name that the website’s
           <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_name_registry" rel="nofollow" style="color: #1155cc;" target="_blank">
            home registry
           </a>
           uses to identify the domain.
          </li>
         </ul>

           Popular Science

            Why Are These Marine Animals Swimming In Circles?
           </a>
          </h2>
         </header>
         <footer class="byline">
          <a class="userlink author-name" href="https://www.neatorama.com/whois/133902/" itemprop="author" rel="author" title="Profile for sodiumnami - Member Since Jun 6th, 2019">

       <section class="postbody">
        <div class="postbody-text">
         <p>
          <noscript>
           <img src="https://uploads.neatorama.com/images/posts/345/118/118345/Why-Are-These-Marine-Animals-Swimming-In-Circles_0-x.jpg"/>
          </noscript>
          <img class="unveil" data-src="https://uploads.neatorama.com/images/posts/345/118/118345/Why-Are-These-Marine-Animals-Swimming-In-Circles_0-x.jpg" src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAAD/ACwAAAAAAQABAAACADs="/>
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="color: rgb(0, 0, 0);">
           Ocean researcher Tomoko Narazaki noticed that a turtle population that they were tracking for a study often swam in circles for no apparent reason. It is more efficient for marine animals to swim in straight lines, and the way these turtles swam as they navigated across the ocean seemed odd, so Narazaki and her colleagues looked at different data from other sea creatures, and to their surprise, found that other than turtles, sharks, seals, penguins, and whales
          </span>
          <a href="https://www.vice.com/en/article/qjpy9m/all-kinds-of-marine-animals-are-mysteriously-circling-in-bizarre-discovery" rel="nofollow" style="color: rgb(17, 85, 204);" target="_blank">
           also swim in circles
          </a>
          <span style="color: rgb(0, 0, 0);">
           :
          </span>
         </p>
         <blockquote>
          <em style="color: rgb(0, 0, 0);">
           The discovery, which was made possible thanks to advances in tagging and 3D-biologging technologies, reveals that swimming in circles is a widespread adaptation that may serve many ecological functions, according to
          </em>
          <a href="https://www.cell.com/iscience/fulltext/S2589-0042(21)00189-9" rel="nofollow" style="color: rgb(17, 85, 204);" target="_blank">
           <em>
            a study published in iScience
           </em>
          </a>
          <em style="color: rgb(0, 0, 0);">
           on Thursday.
          </em>
         </blockquote>
         <blockquote>
          <em style="color: rgb(0, 0, 0);">
           “Analysis of animal tracks provides our understanding of navigation, moving capacity, and internal state of moving animals, as well as identifying the effect of external factors affecting their movements,” said Narazaki and her colleagues in the study.
          </em>
         </blockquote>
         <blockquote>
          <em style="color: rgb(0, 0, 0);">
           “Marine animals move through an inherently 3D environment, yet their movements have been primarily examined in less dimensions,” the team added, a discrepancy that is “due to logistical and technical difficulties primarily derived from seawater’s impermeability to radio waves.”
          </em>
         </blockquote>
         <blockquote>
          <em style="color: rgb(0, 0, 0);">
           In other words, collecting high-resolution information about the motions of marine animals in three dimensions has been an entrenched technological challenge for scientists. But within the past decade or so, the advent of sensors that can record detailed geographic and behavioral data has allowed scientists to hone in on precise features of animal movements such as pitch, heading, and small changes in depth. These biologging innovations now enable scientists to capture behaviors like the circle-swimming described in the new study.
          </em>
         </blockquote>
         <p>
          <em style="color: rgb(0, 0, 0);">
           Image via Vice
          </em>
         </p>

           <a href="https://www.neatorama.com/2021/03/20/Are-Black-Holes-Actually-Black-Holes/" itemprop="name">
            Are Black Holes Actually Black Holes?
           </a>
          </h2>
         </header>
         <footer class="byline">
          <a class="userlink author-name" href="https://www.neatorama.com/whois/133902/" itemprop="author" rel="author" title="Profile for sodiumnami - Member Since Jun 6th, 2019">

       <section class="postbody">
        <div class="postbody-text">
         <p>
          <noscript>
           <img src="https://uploads.neatorama.com/images/posts/347/118/118347/Are-Black-Holes-Actually-Black-Holes_0-x.jpg"/>
          </noscript>
          <img class="unveil" data-src="https://uploads.neatorama.com/images/posts/347/118/118347/Are-Black-Holes-Actually-Black-Holes_0-x.jpg" src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAAD/ACwAAAAAAQABAAACADs="/>
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="color: rgb(0, 0, 0);">
           A new
          </span>
          <a href="https://www.popularmechanics.com/space/deep-space/a35875454/what-are-black-holes-new-theory/" rel="nofollow" style="color: rgb(17, 85, 204);" target="_blank">
           theory
          </a>
          <span style="color: rgb(0, 0, 0);">
           suggests that black holes aren’t black in color, or holes at all! Surprising, I know. According to the theory, black holes may be dark stars with hearts of extremely dense, exotic matter. At the center of a black hole lies what scientists call a singularity, a infinitely small and dense point where the pull of gravity is so strong that it surpasses the speed of light:
          </span>
         </p>
         <blockquote>
          <em style="color: rgb(0, 0, 0);">
           The problem? The singularity appears to be physically impossible, because matter isn’t capable of collapsing into an infinitely small point.
          </em>
         </blockquote>
         <blockquote>
          <em style="color: rgb(0, 0, 0);">
           Physicists have cleverly dodged this issue by inventing their own singularity-free black holes, which they call “dark stars”. These imaginative creations appear like black holes on the outside, but inside, they contain an extremely (but not infinitely) dense core of matter compressed to the tiniest possible scale, or a “Planck core”. It borrows its name from the incredibly small fundamental unit of measurement called the Planck length, which is on the order of 10^-35 meters, or roughly 100 trillion times smaller than a proton.
          </em>
         </blockquote>
         <blockquote>
          <em style="color: rgb(0, 0, 0);">
           Without a singularity at its center, a dark star could theoretically allow light to escape its powerful gravitational grasp. Any light that would escape the black hole would be stretched like a slinky from the dark star’s gravitational pull, an observable phenomenon scientists call redshift.
          </em>
         </blockquote>
         <blockquote>
          <em style="color: rgb(0, 0, 0);">
           “In strong gravitational fields, [dark stars] behave interestingly,” physicist Igor Nikitin, of Germany’s Fraunhofer Institute for Scientific Algorithms and Computing, writes in
          </em>
          <a href="https://arxiv.org/abs/2102.07769" rel="nofollow" style="color: rgb(17, 85, 204);" target="_blank">
           <em>
            his new paper
           </em>
          </a>
          <em style="color: rgb(0, 0, 0);">
           , which appears on the preprint server arXiv:
          </em>
         </blockquote>
         <blockquote>
          <em style="color: rgb(0, 0, 0);">
           “First of all, the event horizon, typical for real black holes, is erased. Instead, a deep gravitational well is formed, where the values of the redshift become enormously large. As a result, for an external observer the star looks black, like a real black hole.”
          </em>
         </blockquote>
         <p>
          <em style="color: rgb(0, 0, 0);">
           Image via Popular Mechanics
          </em>
         </p>

            The History of the Refrigerator
           </a>
          </h2>
         </header>
         <footer class="byline">
          <a class="userlink author-name" href="https://www.neatorama.com/whois/dyeselaaa/" itemprop="author" rel="author" title="Profile for dyeselaaa - Member Since Jun 7th, 2019">

       </div>
       <section class="postbody">
        <div class="postbody-text">
         <p>
          <noscript>
           <img alt="" src="https://uploads.neatorama.com/images/posts/333/118/118333/History-of-Refrigerator_0-x.jpg"/>
          </noscript>
          <img alt="" class="unveil" data-src="https://uploads.neatorama.com/images/posts/333/118/118333/History-of-Refrigerator_0-x.jpg" src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAAD/ACwAAAAAAQABAAACADs="/>
         </p>
         <p>
          Can you imagine a life without a refrigerator? In the part of the world where I'm in, it's already summer and I can't live without
          <em>
           very
          </em>
          cold water. Can't imagine life without the fridge! So, here's a bit of history for all of us.
         </p>
         <blockquote>
          The concept of mechanical refrigeration began when William Cullen, a Scottish doctor, observed that evaporation had a cooling effect in the 1720s. He demonstrated his ideas in 1748 by evaporating ethyl ether in a vacuum, according to Peak Mechanical Partnership, a plumbing and heating company based in Saskatoon, Saskatchewan.
         </blockquote>
         <blockquote>
          Oliver Evans, an American inventor, designed but did not build a refrigeration machine that used vapor instead of liquid in 1805. In 1820, English scientist Michael Faraday used liquefied ammonia to cause cooling.
         </blockquote>
         <blockquote>
          Jacob Perkins, who worked with Evans, received a patent for a vapor-compression cycle using liquid ammonia in 1835, according to History of Refrigeration. For that, he is sometimes called the "
          <strong>
           father of the refrigerator
          </strong>
          ."
         </blockquote>
         <blockquote>
          John Gorrie, an America doctor, also built a machine similar to Evans' design in 1842. Gorrie used his refrigerator, which created ice, to cool down patients with yellow fever in a Florida hospital. Gorrie received the first U.S. patent for his method of artificially creating ice in 1851.
         </blockquote>
         <p>
          Read more about how refrigerators work and the future of refrigerators
          <a href="https://www.livescience.com/57797-refrigerator-history.html" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
           here
          </a>
          .
         </p>

           <a href="https://www.neatorama.com/2021/03/19/The-Mystery-of-Harriet-Cole/" itemprop="name">
            The Mystery of ‘Harriet Cole’
           </a>
          </h2>
         </header>
         <footer class="byline">
          <a class="userlink author-name" href="https://www.neatorama.com/whois/miss-cellania/" itemprop="author" rel="author" title="Profile for Miss Cellania - Member Since Aug 4th, 2012">

       <section class="postbody">
        <div class="postbody-text">
         <p>
          <iframe frameborder="0" height="650" src="//instagram.com/p/CGnCuwjla1y/embed/" width="600">
          </iframe>
          <br/>
          <br/>
          Rufus Weaver was an anatomist of the late 19th century. His specialty was preparing anatomical samples, that is, preserving human body parts and organs for medical classes at Hahnemann Medical Collage. Weaver's most famous project was the retrieval, preservation, and display of an entire human nervous system, which had never been done before. In 1888, Weaver dissected a body and separated out the nervous system: nerves, brain, and eyeballs, and preserved it in the preparation you see here. The specimen is unnerving to anyone who sees it, and you have to wonder who this was in life. It wasn't until 1915 that she was identified as "Harriet Cole," a black woman who worked at the college, and was said to have willed her body to science.
         </p>
         <blockquote>
          <p>
           When another Hahnemann physician, George Geckeler, restored the mounted model in 1960, LIFE magazine devoted a splashy photo spread to the effort. The writer recounted how a scrubwoman who had been ignored by everyone in the laboratory “stared in fascination at cadavers” and “eavesdropp[ed]” on lectures. She osmosed the chatter, the author continued; Harriet supposedly “took to heart [Weaver’s] complaints about a shortage of corpses” and “willed her body to him.” There’s no indication of how the writer gleaned this information—this supposedly intimate understanding of a long-dead woman’s perceptions, thoughts, feelings, and behaviors. It seems that “no one went back to fact-check” the basic story, Herbison says. “Whoever said it first, that’s the thing that you use.” Details carry over from one story to another like sprawling arithmetic.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           One photograph accompanying the LIFE story shows Geckeler stooping so his eyes align with the ones on the preparation. He scrunches his own, like someone puzzling over a painting, studying the canvas as if trying to decipher genius suspended between the fibers. By this point, the myth of “Harriet Cole” had grown to include not just Weaver’s work, but the woman herself—a lowly person made spectacular, in every sense. A fascinating object never quite or fully human, but almost looking the part.
          </p>
         </blockquote>
         <p>
          That story is hard to believe, considering that willing one's body to science was not a thing in 1888. Cadavers for medical specimens at the time came from grave robbing and claiming bodies that would otherwise be buried by the state. Alaina McNaughton, Matt Herbison, and Brandon Zimmerman have been trying to track down the mystery of Harriet Cole. Was she a real person, and was she the owner of this nervous system? Weaver left few clues as to how he prepared the specimen, and the identity of the donor was not in his records. Read what the team found out about Weaver's work and
          <a href="https://www.atlasobscura.com/articles/harriet-cole-human-nervous-system-philadelphia" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
           the person whose nerves are still on display 130 years later
          </a>
          at Atlas Obscura.
         </p>
